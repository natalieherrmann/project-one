Relevant Coursework

Of course, I've taken more classes than those listed below! These courses had features most relevant to my interests and goals.

PYSC 381: Human Issues in Technology

As the title suggests, this class focused on how technology impacts our life. Notable topics that we discussed pertaining to technology's ever increasing presence include the fear of missing out (FOMO), online dating, privacy and surveillance, smart technology design, and technological communication's impact on empathy, language, and relationships.

COM 371: Persuasion

After learning about persuasion and propaganda throughout the semester, I focused on persuasive web design for my final research paper. As there are millions of websites on the Internet, each website must have a design that persuades users to stay on their site. While aesthetics, credibility, and usability were key features of persuasive web design, dark patterns were one of the most interesting things I learned about. Dark patterns include tricks such as hiding or burying "No" or "Delete Account" buttons, making confusing opt-in/opt-out selections for marketing emails, and disguising ads to garner clicks.

Ultimately, a website's design should be persuasive enough to deter you from hitting the back button, but a site that uses dark patterns may redirect you so many times the back button never lets you out!

HUM 372: Interactive Storytelling

This course focused on elements of worldbuilding, character design, and storytelling in relation to interactive mediums (e.g. video games). I enjoyed learning how to use the affordances of a particular medium and consider the user/player when designing an interactive experience.

For our final project, we worked in groups to create a game about a historical event in Chicago using Twine. You can play my group's game about the Chicago's World Fair here!

Interprofessional Projects (IPRO): Designed for Silence

The purpose of this IPRO was to find ways for people to disconnect from technology and form a healthier, balanced relationship with the tech in their life. As a class, we focused on designing spaces in cities around the world that would allow people to get a break from technology. In smaller groups, we concentrated on particular cities.

My group's city was Tokyo. After researching technological usage and culture in Tokyo, we came up with ideas that could improve already-existing cat cafes to promote opportunities to disconnect and de-stress.

At the Spring 2018 IPRO day, my whole class won the Dean's Choice Award from the School of Architecture.
